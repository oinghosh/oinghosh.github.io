Bio

I am a Postdoctoral Associate in Civil and Environmental Engineering at the University of Pittsburgh,working with Dr. Carla Ng Baumel. I study exposure pathways and health risks of persistent and emerging pollutants,including PFAS. My work sits at the intersection of environmental chemistry, toxicokinetic modeling,and community-engaged research. I currently contribute to projects assessing PFAS and VOC/SVOC exposures in communities impacted by industrial activities and accidental spills, using in-home sampling, personal wearable samplers, and targeted mass spectrometric analysis (LC-MS/MS, GC-MS). I also train students and community partners in rigorous, EPA-guided sampling and analytical methods.

I received my PhD in Environmental Engineering from the University of Maryland, Baltimore County, where I developed and optimized equilibrium passive sampling methods for surface water and porewater under the guidance of Dr. Upal Ghosh. I am passionate about environmental health and exposure assessment, with a focus on understanding the fundamental mechanisms that govern pollutant mobility through the environment. By tracing how contaminants move, transform, and accumulate, I seek to better understand the critical links between environmental pollution and human and ecological health. I develop and refine methods to accurately measure pollutant bioavailability, using an integrated approach that combines mathematical modeling, field sampling, and laboratory experiments.

Alongside my research, I am deeply committed to science communication. Through writing and illustration, I aim to make complex environmental health topics accessible to diverse audiences beyond academia. This creative practice allows me to translate data into compelling narratives, while continually developing my skills as a science writer and illustrator with a focus on clarity, accuracy, and impact.
